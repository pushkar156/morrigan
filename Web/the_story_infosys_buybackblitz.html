Infosys’s ₹18,000 Crore Buyback: Financial Engineering or Strategic Confidence?

Bengaluru – Infosys Ltd., India's second-largest software exporter, is leaning heavily into one of corporate finance's bluntest instruments: the share buyback. The company last week approved a ₹18,000 crore ($2.04 billion) repurchase programme – its largest yet – showing off both its financial heft and the uncertainty faced by the wider information-technology group.

The buyback, which will take place via a tender offer at ₹1,800 per share, is a 19% premium to Infosys's trading price before the announcement, and will retire 10 crore shares, or around 2.41% of its equity base.

What Sparked the Buy-Back

1. Excess Cash, Few Alternatives

Infosys has produced more strong free cash flow than it knows what to do with—$884 million (₹7,805 crore) in the June 2025 quarter alone. For a company with little debt and few near-term potential large-scale acquisitions, it has developed a culture centred on returning cash. Management has guided to returning 85% of cumulative free cash flow through dividends and buybacks.

2. A Signal of Confidence

By paying a double-digit premium, Infosys is signaling that it believes the market is undervaluing its stock. A buyback is the corporate equivalent of saying: “We’re better than the market realises.”

3. Boosting EPS and ROE Math

Shrinking the share count affects per-share financial metrics. If net income remains flat, Earnings Per Share (EPS) grows. Simultaneously, with less equity capital, Return on Equity (ROE) improves. Sceptics often view this as financial engineering rather than organic growth.

Same Playbook — Bigger Scale

Since 2017, Infosys has executed five major buybacks:

  • 2017: ₹13,000 crore
  • 2019: ₹8,260 crore
  • 2021: ₹9,200 crore
  • 2022: ₹9,300 crore
  • 2025: ₹18,000 crore (Largest ever)

What Shareholders Should Consider

  • Tendering versus Holding: Tendering does not guarantee acceptance; it depends on the total participation. Small shareholders (up to ₹2 lakh) often receive higher acceptance priority.
  • Tax Implications: Buyback proceeds are taxed as capital gains in India. The actual after-tax return may be lower than the 19% premium.
  • Long-Term Growth Trade-Offs: Cash returned to investors is cash not reinvested in R&D or AI acquisitions.

Industry and Global Context

The IT services industry faces a prolonged slowdown. In this climate, handing cash back instead of parking it on the balance sheet is seen as capital discipline. Globally, Infosys is following the "buyback machine" playbook used by giants like Apple and Microsoft, though critics argue those U.S. firms spend significantly more on innovation alongside their repurchases.

Risks Investors Can't Overlook

  • Timing Risk: If the macro economy worsens, the company may have overpaid for its own stock.
  • Opportunity Cost: Restricted funds for emerging tech like AI, cybersecurity, or cloud platforms.
  • Regulatory Scrutiny: India’s new buyback rules (2024) impose stricter disclosure requirements.

The Big Takeaway

This buyback acts as both a reward and a narrative. It tells the market: "We have lots of cash, we are disciplined, and we are confident."

Regardless, the ₹18,000 crore move marks a shift. Infosys—once the poster child of rapid rise—is now behaving like a global blue chip: conservative, shareholder-friendly, and cautious in a world that lacks confidence.
