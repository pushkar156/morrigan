Wakefit IPO: From Sleep Specialist to Home Solutions Giant

Company Overview

Imagine starting a venture in the small corner of Bengaluru, selling just one thing: mattresses. A few years later, the same company is launching an IPO (Initial Public Offering) to become a prominent player in the home market. That company is known as Wakefit Innovations Limited – expanding from a “sleep specialist” to a full-blown “home solutions giant”.

Founded in 2016, Ankit Garg and Chaitanya Ramalingegowda identified a fragmented mattress market and set out to provide affordable solutions. It is a D2C business operating through websites and apps, as well as COCO stores (company-owned, company-operated). Serving over 9.9 million orders and a vision to reach 2 lakh crore organised homes, the company is poised to enter the next phase: expanding to 100+ stores and penetrating Tier 2 and Tier 3 markets.

Structure of the Offer

  • Fresh Issue: A fresh issue of 4,682 crores is used to repay debt, fund expansion, and provide working capital for growth.
  • Offer for Sale (OFS): Offer for sale of 5.2 crores shares, to be sold by existing shareholders. This includes investors such as Peak XV (25 million shares) and the two founders, Ankit Garg (7.7 M) and Chaitanya (4.5 M).
  • Allocation: The offer is 100% Book-Built, with 75% allocated to Institutional investors, 15% to high-net-worth individuals, and 10% to retail customers.

Risks Associated

  • Concentration Risk: A large share of revenue comes from mattresses. Changes in customer tastes or new competitors could significantly impact the company.
  • Supply Chain Vulnerability: The company has no long-term contracts with suppliers and meets demand through spot purchases. Price hikes or shortages could halt production.
  • Logistics Dependency: Wakefit does not have its own logistics and depends on third-party services. Cost increases could impact customer satisfaction.
  • Liquidity Risk: Significant lease liabilities and trade payables create a potential cash crunch in the event of an emergency.
  • Interest Rate Risk: Any increase in interest rates would raise finance costs due to higher borrowings.
